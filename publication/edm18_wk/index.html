Investigating How School Aggregated Data Can Influence in Predicting STEM Career from Student usage of an Intelligent Tutoring System

Jihed Makhlouf, Tsunenori Mine
July 2018
PDF Project
Image credit: Educational Data Mining

Abstract

For several years the Science, Technology, Engineering and Maths (STEM) fields have been growing and continue to grow rapidly. Recent reports in the United States show that growth in STEM employment has increased to 1.7% per year, while non-STEM positions grew by 0.6% per year during the same period of time. Efforts have been made to encourage people to pursue a career in STEM fields. Nevertheless, the provision of the skills students require to be capable of pursuing a STEM career starts earlier than college. In fact, students need to be trained from middle school, as it is then that students make their choices. Thanks to the increasing adoption of educational software, detecting students' problems with maths and science subjects has become more efficient. And more recently, models using data gathered from an Intelligent Tutoring System have become capable of predicting STEM or non-STEM college major enrollment. In this paper, we focus on predicting STEM field career choice using longitudinal data from an Intelligent Tutoring System. We investigate if comparing student data to that of their peer school-mates can improve the prediction models. We find that aggregating student levels within the same schools improves prediction models and leads to better AUC scores (0.601) compared to models using student data without this school-based transformation.
